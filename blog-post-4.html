Cord blood and the banking model

Public and private cord blood banks now hold an estimated five million units worldwide, with cross-border releases — where a stored unit is shipped to a transplant centre in another country — growing at roughly 18% per year. Each release is a one-way, irreversible shipment: if the unit is compromised in transit, there is no second sample. The margin for logistics error is zero.

iPSC therapies and the personalised medicine pipeline

Unlike cord blood, which is banked at birth and retrieved years later, iPSC-based therapies are manufactured to order — cells are harvested from the patient, reprogrammed, differentiated into the target tissue type, and shipped back to the treating clinic. The manufacturing site and the treatment site are frequently in different countries, making each therapy dependent on at least two international cold-chain shipments per patient.

Regulatory frameworks have not yet caught up with the speed of this development: in many jurisdictions, iPSC products occupy an ambiguous space between a pharmaceutical and a tissue graft, creating customs classification challenges that specialist couriers must navigate case by case.

What this means for reproductive medicine

The operational overlap between reproductive cryogenics and regenerative medicine logistics is larger than it appears. Both require vapor-phase liquid nitrogen transport. Both demand unbroken chain-of-custody documentation. Both carry specimens that cannot be replaced if lost. Clinics and biotech companies investing in regenerative pipelines are increasingly looking to reproductive-medicine courier networks — which have decades of hard-won experience with these exact constraints — as the infrastructure layer for their cell therapy logistics.
